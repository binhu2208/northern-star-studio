# Emotion Cards Four — v1 Pitch Variants

Used for audience validation testing. Each script is designed to land in 10–20 seconds of spoken delivery, max 30 seconds.

---

## Variant 1: Social-Empathy Framing

**Script:**
> "Emotion Cards Four is a card game about understanding what people really feel — reading between the lines, picking up on the stuff people don't say out loud. If you've ever misread a room, or wished you knew what someone was actually thinking, this is the game for you."

**What it's testing:**
- **Audience segment:** Adults 25–45, leans emotionally intelligent / self-aware. People who enjoy podcasts about psychology, relationships, or human behavior.
- **Framing angle:** Empathy as a skill — not just "fun cards," but *practice reading people*. Appeals to curiosity about human nature.
- **Hypothesis:** This framing will resonate with folks who already see themselves as perceptive and want a game that rewards that.

**When to use:**
- Early testing with unknown audiences
- Anyone who mentions being "good with people" or enjoying conversation-heavy games
- Cold outreach where you need a single hook

---

## Variant 2: Readable Social Drama Framing

**Script:**
> "It's like Cards Against Humanity meets The Relationship Dinner Party — a card game full of awkward situations, messy feelings, and the kind of conversations you usually avoid. Things get real. Things get funny. You end up knowing people better by the end of the night."

**What it's testing:**
- **Audience segment:** Ages 21–35, social drinkers/game-night crowd, party game fans who want something with more bite.
- **Framing angle:** Social drama as entertainment. The appeal is vicarious — watching people squirm, laugh, and open up.
- **Hypothesis:** "Drama" is a hook. This framing works because it promises *moments*, not mechanics.

**When to use:**
- Party/game night contexts
- audiences who loved CAH or similar
- Anyone who reacts to "things get REAL" energy

---

## Variant 3: Scenario-Plus-Choice Framing

**Script:**
> "Emotion Cards Four gives you real situations — a hard conversation, a tense moment, a relationship crossroads — and you pick what you'd actually do. Then you find out how others handled it. It's part card game, part looking in a mirror with your friends."

**What it's testing:**
- **Audience segment:** Strategy-literate players, RPG/board game enthusiasts, people who like games with meaningful choices.
- **Framing angle:** Agency and self-discovery. The value is in *your choices* and comparing them to others.
- **Hypothesis:** Players who want more than luck-based games will gravitate toward this — it signals depth without being heavy.

**When to use:**
- Board game café or hobbyist store settings
- TTRPG or D&D adjacent crowds
- Anyone who asks "how do you play?" before "what's it about?"

---

## Variant 4: Group Conversation Framing

**Script:**
> "You know that game where everyone goes around and says what they'd do in some wild scenario? This is that — but the cards are sharper, the situations are ripped from real life, and your friends are funnier than you expected. Perfect for four players or more, minimum effort, maximum conversation."

**What it's testing:**
- **Audience segment:** Groups looking for low-prep social activity. Couples' game night, friend groups, icebreakers.
- **Framing angle:** Easy social glue. The promise is effortless connection, not a learning curve.
- **Hypothesis:** This sells *the moment*, not the game. Works for people who want to feel closer to their group without doing emotional homework.

**When to use:**
- Low-stakes social settings (house parties, first dates, team events)
- Anyone who says "we're looking for something to play as a group"
- When the audience is more focused on *who they're playing with* than the game itself

---

## Testing Notes

| Variant | Best for | Key fear to test |
|---------|----------|-----------------|
| Social-Empathy | Introspective / relationship-focused | Does "empathy" sound too self-helpy? |
| Social Drama | Party energy / existing game fans | Does "drama" sound exhausting or cliquey? |
| Scenario-Choice | Strategy / choice-lovers | Does it sound like homework? |
| Group Conversation | Casual / low-effort seekers | Does "minimum effort" undersell the game? |

Test by showing the script and asking one question: *"Does this make you want to play?"* — then shut up and listen.
