# Emotion Cards Four — Market Brief

## One-Line Hook
A replayable social-empathy card game where players use emotion, memory, and reaction cards to shape not just the outcome, but how the situation is understood.

## Audience Segments

### Core Audience
- **Social strategy players** who want more meaning and personality than standard party-card play
- **Narrative-curious players** who like interpretation, relationships, and readable consequences without committing to a heavy story game
- **Creator-friendly groups** looking for games that generate watchable choices, table talk, and emotionally legible moments

### Secondary Audience
- Players who enjoy cozy or reflective themes but still want clear game structure
- Couples, friends, and small groups looking for conversation-rich replayability
- Players burned out on pure deception or trivia party games and open to something more thoughtful

## Audience Framing to Use
Lead with **social-empathy card game** first.

Do **not** lead with therapy, self-help, or education. Those may be adjacent interpretations, but they weaken the commercial read if positioned as the core product.

## Positioning Statement
**Emotion Cards Four** sits between social card play and empathy-driven narrative play: more emotionally readable than strategy-first card games, more replayable than one-and-done reflective experiences, and more watchable than private journaling-style emotional tools.

## Flagship Mode Summary

### Flagship Promise
A scenario-based social mode where players respond to emotional situations using cards from three readable layers:
- **Emotion** — what is being felt
- **Memory** — what history shapes the moment
- **Reaction** — how the player responds now

### Messaging Focus
The flagship mode should be described as:
- easy to understand in one round
- expressive in how different players approach the same situation
- replayable because emotional combinations create different outcomes
- socially readable for both players and spectators

### What to Avoid
Do not pitch it as:
- a hardcore deckbuilder
- a therapeutic product
- an educational curriculum
- an anything-goes emotional sandbox

The sell is **structured social interpretation**, not breadth.

## Validation Checklist
Prototype validation should answer yes to most of the following:

- Can new players understand the card roles quickly?
- Can players explain why a card worked or failed in emotional terms?
- Do two runs of the same scenario feel meaningfully different?
- Can an observer understand the stakes and choices without extra explanation?
- Does the game feel engaging without sounding preachy or emotionally heavy-handed?
- Do players describe it more as a game they want to replay than a one-time curiosity?
- Is the strongest pull clearly **social play**, with reflective depth as a bonus rather than the main sell?

## Messaging Guardrails

### Lean Into
- social-empathy
- readable emotional stakes
- replayable scenarios
- expressive card combinations
- conversation-rich outcomes
- accessible but not shallow

### Avoid
- therapy language
- moralizing language
- “learn emotions” framing
- overly clinical or wellness-product wording
- promising deep narrative drama if the prototype is still system-led
- abstract claims that do not map to actual play

### Tone Standard
Grounded, human, and specific.
The product should feel emotionally smart, not emotionally self-important.

## Recommended Immediate Next Steps
1. Lock the external-facing category language to **social-empathy card game**.
2. Write 3-5 short pitch variants and test which framing gets the fastest understanding.
3. Define audience-fit playtests that compare:
   - social or party players
   - narrative or reflective players
   - mixed groups
4. Create a simple observer test for creator/readability value: can someone watching one round explain what happened?
5. Build prototype feedback questions around replay intent, clarity, tone, and audience pull.
6. Capture one ideal flagship-mode scenario example for future store, trailer, and internal alignment use.
