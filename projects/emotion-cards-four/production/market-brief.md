# Emotion Cards Four — Market Brief

## One-Line Hook
A replayable social-empathy card game where players use emotion, memory, and reaction cards to shape not just the outcome, but how the situation is understood.

## Audience Segments

### Core Audience
- **Social strategy players** who want more meaning and personality than standard party-card play
- **Narrative-curious players** who like interpretation, relationships, and readable consequences without committing to a heavy story game
- **Creator-friendly groups** looking for games that generate watchable choices, table talk, and emotionally legible moments

### Secondary Audience
- Players who enjoy cozy or reflective themes but still want clear game structure
- Couples, friends, and small groups looking for conversation-rich replayability
- Players burned out on pure deception or trivia party games and open to something more thoughtful

## Audience Framing to Use
Lead with **social-empathy card game** first.

Do **not** lead with therapy, self-help, or education. Those may be adjacent interpretations, but they weaken the commercial read if positioned as the core product.

## Positioning Statement
**Emotion Cards Four** sits between social card play and empathy-driven narrative play: more emotionally readable than strategy-first card games, more replayable than one-and-done reflective experiences, and more watchable than private journaling-style emotional tools.

## Flagship Mode Summary

### Flagship Promise
A scenario-based social mode where players respond to emotional situations using cards from three readable layers:
- **Emotion** — what is being felt
- **Memory** — what history shapes the moment
- **Reaction** — how the player responds now

### Messaging Focus
The flagship mode should be described as:
- easy to understand in one round
- expressive in how different players approach the same situation
- replayable because emotional combinations create different outcomes
- socially readable for both players and spectators

### What to Avoid
Do not pitch it as:
- a hardcore deckbuilder
- a therapeutic product
- an educational curriculum
- an anything-goes emotional sandbox

The sell is **structured social interpretation**, not breadth.

## Validation Checklist
Prototype validation should answer yes to most of the following:

- Can new players understand the card roles quickly?
- Can players explain why a card worked or failed in emotional terms?
- Do two runs of the same scenario feel meaningfully different?
- Can an observer understand the stakes and choices without extra explanation?
- Does the game feel engaging without sounding preachy or emotionally heavy-handed?
- Do players describe it more as a game they want to replay than a one-time curiosity?
- Is the strongest pull clearly **social play**, with reflective depth as a bonus rather than the main sell?

## Messaging Guardrails

### Lean Into
- social-empathy
- readable emotional stakes
- replayable scenarios
- expressive card combinations
- conversation-rich outcomes
- accessible but not shallow

### Avoid
- therapy language
- moralizing language
- "learn emotions" framing
- overly clinical or wellness-product wording
- promising deep narrative drama if the prototype is still system-led
- abstract claims that do not map to actual play

### Tone Standard
Grounded, human, and specific.
The product should feel emotionally smart, not emotionally self-important.

## Recommended Immediate Next Steps
1. Lock the external-facing category language to **social-empathy card game**.
2. Write 3-5 short pitch variants and test which framing gets the fastest understanding.
3. Define audience-fit playtests that compare:
   - social or party players
   - narrative or reflective players
   - mixed groups
4. Create a simple observer test for creator/readability value: can someone watching one round explain what happened?
5. Build prototype feedback questions around replay intent, clarity, tone, and audience pull.
6. Capture one ideal flagship-mode scenario example for future store, trailer, and internal alignment use.

## v1 Audience-Fit Validation Path

### What Changed After Prototype
The prototype milestone proved:
- The core draw/play/discard loop works and is runnable
- QA's validation pipeline catches real issues (Issue #11 runtime bug was found and fixed)
- Issue #12 (UX readability gap) is a bounded v1 requirement, not prototype polish
- The core interaction is validated; what v1 now needs to prove is **audience pull and message fit at scale**

Prototype validation answered: "does the game work?"
v1 validation must now answer: "do the right players want it, and can we reach them?"

### v1 Validation Objectives
1. Confirm which audience segment responds strongest and most consistently to the social-empathy framing
2. Prove the pitch language creates accurate expectations without over-promising
3. Establish whether the game earns replay intent after a single session
4. Identify whether the current positioning holds or needs adjustment before broader outreach
5. Generate evidence for store page, creator outreach, and press positioning

### What v1 Must Prove
- **Audience pull:** the right players self-identify as interested without requiring a long explanation
- **Pitch accuracy:** players who try it describe it close to how it was marketed
- **Replay signal:** players want to go again without prompting
- **Segment clarity:** we know which audience is lead and which is secondary
- **Creator/readability viability:** observers understand what is happening without gameplay context

### Success Criteria
A v1 audience validation pass is successful when:
- At least one audience segment shows 70%+ positive interest signal across 10+ respondents
- Pitch explanation produces accurate expectations in 80%+ of respondents
- 50%+ of playtest participants request another session unprompted
- No segment registers the product primarily as therapy, education, or wellness
- An observer can describe the game state and stakes correctly after watching one round

### Fail Signals
- Strong interest only comes after extended team explanation
- Respondents consistently misinterpret the product category
- Replay intent is below 30% after first session
- The primary interested audience is too narrow to support commercial targets
- Therapy or educational framing keeps surfacing unprompted

### Recommended Test Format
Keep v1 validation lean and direct:
- **12-20 concept/pitch tests** across 3 audience segments using the four pitch variants from the prototype validation plan
- **5-8 prototype playtests** with post-session debrief using the same question set
- **Observer readability check** during play: can a non-player explain what is happening and why?
- **A/B pitch framing** where two different introduction scripts are used and response quality is compared

### v1 Validation Deliverables
After the validation round, produce:
1. **Audience response summary** — ranked segments by interest strength and accuracy of expectation
2. **Pitch scorecard** — which variant performed best on clarity, interest, and accuracy
3. **Objection and confusion log** — recurring questions, misinterpretations, and hesitation patterns
4. **Replay intent report** — session follow-up data on whether participants want to continue
5. **Observer readability notes** — whether non-players could follow the game state at a glance
6. **Revised one-line hook and 30-second pitch** — refined based on what actually landed

### How v1 Differs from Prototype Validation
| | Prototype Validation | v1 Validation |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Does the core loop work? | Do the right players want it? |
| **Primary question** | Can players play it? | Will the right audience seek it out? |
| **Test type** | Functional playtest | Audience-fit and pitch accuracy |
| **Success bar** | Game runs, mechanics function | Players self-select correctly, replay intent forms |
| **Output** | Bug reports, UX gaps | Segment priority, pitch refinement, market positioning |

### Immediate v1 Validation Next Steps
1. Lock the four pitch variants from the prototype validation plan as the test candidates
2. Recruit 4-6 respondents per audience segment (social strategy, narrative/reflective, mixed casual)
3. Run pitch tests before any gameplay exposure
4. Run playtests with post-session debrief using the prototype question set
5. Capture observer readability notes during live play
6. Publish findings within one week of testing start
