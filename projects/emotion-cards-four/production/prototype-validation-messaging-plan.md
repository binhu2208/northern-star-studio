# Emotion Cards Four — Prototype Validation Messaging Plan

## Purpose
Validate whether **Emotion Cards Four** has a clear audience, a strong enough hook, and pitch language that makes people want to try it now.

This round is meant to answer three practical questions:
- **Audience fit:** who responds fastest and most positively to the concept?
- **Message fit:** which framing creates immediate understanding and interest?
- **Hook refinement:** which short pitch best earns a playtest, wishlist, follow-up, or share?

## What This Validation Round Must Produce
1. A ranked view of the best-fit audience segments
2. A ranked list of pitch and hook variants by response quality
3. Clear evidence of what language causes confusion, resistance, or false expectations
4. A revised short pitch, one-line hook, and test script for the next prototype round

## Test Audiences

### 1) Core Social Strategy Players
People who like social card games, party games, bluffing, deduction, or expressive table talk.

**Why test them**
- They are the most commercially relevant first audience
- They will quickly reveal whether the game sounds playable or vague

### 2) Narrative / Reflective Players
People who like story-rich, relationship-driven, cozy, or emotionally readable games.

**Why test them**
- They may strongly respond to the empathy and interpretation layer
- They help test whether the concept feels meaningful without sounding heavy-handed

### 3) Mixed Casual Groups
Friends, couples, or small groups with uneven gaming depth.

**Why test them**
- They are a good reality check for onboarding clarity
- They expose whether the pitch is too niche, too abstract, or too designer-facing

## Core Test Questions
Use the same base questions across interviews, landing-page tests, and live pitch tests.

### Audience-Fit Questions
- Who do you think this game is for?
- Does this sound like something you would play with your usual group? Why or why not?
- Does it sound more like a party game, a social strategy game, a reflective game, or something else?
- What kind of group would enjoy this most?
- What kind of group would bounce off it?

### Message-Clarity Questions
- After hearing the pitch once, what do you think players actually do?
- What part feels immediately clear?
- What part feels fuzzy or hard to picture?
- Does any wording make it sound like therapy, education, or self-help?
- What expectation would you have going into the first round?

### Hook / Interest Questions
- Would you try this based on that pitch alone?
- What is the most interesting part?
- What is the main reason you might skip it?
- Which version makes you most curious?
- Which version sounds most replayable?

## Pitch Variants To Trial
Keep each pitch short enough to say out loud in 10–20 seconds.

### Variant A — Social-Empathy Framing
**Emotion Cards Four is a social-empathy card game where players respond to emotional situations using emotion, memory, and reaction cards.**

**What it tests**
- Whether “social-empathy” is clear and compelling
- Whether the card-layer structure helps understanding

### Variant B — Readable Social Drama Framing
**A replayable card game about reading the moment, revealing emotional context, and choosing how to respond under pressure.**

**What it tests**
- Whether outcome drama lands better than category language
- Whether the concept feels watchable and interpretable

### Variant C — Scenario + Choice Framing
**Players face emotional scenarios and play cards that shape what the moment means, what history matters, and how it ends.**

**What it tests**
- Whether scenario structure sells better than genre/category language
- Whether the “shape the moment” promise feels distinctive

### Variant D — Group Conversation Framing
**A card game for groups who want more meaning than a typical party game, without turning the night into therapy.**

**What it tests**
- Whether contrast positioning works better than mechanic explanation
- Whether anti-therapy framing reduces hesitation

## Evaluation Criteria
Score each audience and pitch combination on a simple 1–5 scale.

- **Clarity:** people understand the game quickly
- **Interest:** people want to try it
- **Audience pull:** the right people self-identify fast
- **Replayability perception:** it sounds like more than a one-off novelty
- **Tone fit:** emotionally smart, not preachy, clinical, or awkward
- **Expectation accuracy:** the pitch matches what the prototype actually delivers

## Success Signals

### Strong Positive Signals
- People can explain the game back in roughly correct terms after one pitch
- People say some version of “I’d try that” without extra rescue explanation
- One or two audience segments consistently show stronger pull than the rest
- A pitch variant produces both high curiosity and accurate understanding
- Players describe it as social, expressive, replayable, or conversation-rich
- Few people interpret it primarily as therapy, wellness, or education

### Warning / Fail Signals
- People repeatedly ask whether it is a therapy tool or emotional self-help product
- Interest only appears after a long explanation from the team
- Different listeners form wildly different expectations of what the game is
- The strongest response is “interesting idea” but not “I want to play it”
- Replayability sounds weak or unclear
- The most interested audience is too narrow to support current goals

## Deliverables / Artifacts To Produce

### Audience-Fit Outputs
- 1-page audience response summary by segment
- Ranked list of strongest-fit and weakest-fit audience groups
- Notes on where each segment got confused or leaned in

### Message-Test Outputs
- Scorecard of all pitch variants across clarity, interest, and expectation accuracy
- Top objections and confusing phrases
- Recommended language to keep, cut, and rewrite

### Hook-Refinement Outputs
- Final recommended one-line hook
- Final 30-second spoken pitch
- Short test script for future playtest recruiting or landing-page use
- 3–5 pull quotes or paraphrased reactions worth reusing internally

## Recommended Test Format
Keep this lightweight.

- **5–8 quick concept interviews per audience segment**
- **A/B pitch testing** in short live calls, DM outreach, or lightweight landing-page copy tests
- **Prototype session debriefs** immediately after play with the same question set
- **Observer notes** on which moments people reference when they describe the game back

## Immediate Next Steps
1. Lock the four pitch variants above into a single test sheet.
2. Recruit testers across the three audience buckets.
3. Run the first 10–15 message tests before changing the prototype pitch again.
4. Capture responses in one shared scoring sheet with the criteria above.
5. Choose the best-performing pitch and rewrite it once, not repeatedly mid-test.
6. Use the winning pitch in the next prototype playtest invite and compare conversion or acceptance rate.
7. After the first round, publish a short findings memo with:
   - best-fit audience
   - strongest pitch
   - biggest confusion points
   - recommended messaging changes
