# Flower Boat — Visual Direction

## Document Overview
- **Project:** Flower Boat
- **Task:** Visual direction, mood references, color palette, and style guidance
- **Status:** Draft for team review
- **Basis:** Core game feel — cozy canal-cruising flower delivery, reading people, "you noticed them"

---

## 1. Core Visual Feel

Flower Boat is a cozy, warm, observational game. The visual language should feel like:
- a quiet afternoon on the water
- the kind of game you play when you're tired and want something gentle
- the satisfaction of noticing something about someone

**What it should NOT feel like:**
- fast-paced or competitive
- bright or garish
- heavily stylized or cartoonish in a loud way
- stressful or high-stakes

---

## 2. Style Reference

**Target aesthetic:** Flat illustration with warmth — think Unpacking, A Short Hike, or Tiny Glade. Clean shapes, soft edges, warm atmosphere. Not cel-shaded, not hyper-realistic.

**Key qualities:**
- Gentle and unhurried — nothing screams for attention
- Warm color temperature — this is a game played in good lighting, not a dark cave
- Simple shapes with enough detail to feel intentional, not sparse
- The scene should feel like you want to be inside it

---

## 3. Color Palette Direction

### Primary palette — warm and natural
| Role | Color | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|
| Canal water | dusty blue-green | Like a canal in the Netherlands or Venice at midday — not neon, not murky |
| Boat wood | warm honey brown | Teak, not pine — aged, maintained, lived-in |
| Sky / backdrop | soft cream to pale gold | Morning warmth, not harsh afternoon glare |
| Foliage | sage green + olive | Restrained greens that don't shout |
| Flowers | warm coral, dusty rose, soft gold | Saturated enough to pop against the muted background |
| UI accents | faded terracotta or warm rust | Anchoring color for buttons and text |

### Weather palette shifts
- **Sunny:** cream sky, bright water, high warmth
- **Rain:** slate gray sky, darker water, cooler overall tone, warmer accents pop more
- **Spring morning:** pale pink sky, mint water, lightest palette
- **Fall afternoon:** amber sky, bronze water, richest warm tone

The weather shift should feel like a mood change, not just a filter swap.

### Text and UI
- Primary text: deep warm charcoal, not pure black
- Secondary text: warm gray
- Avoid: pure white backgrounds, harsh contrast

---

## 4. Typography Direction

**Primary:** A warm, readable serif for titles — something like Fraunces or Lora. Friendly but not whimsical.

**Secondary:** A clean, legible sans for body text and UI — something like Atkinson Hyperlegible or Source Sans Pro. Comfortable for reading at any size.

**Don't use:**
- Cold or clinical sans-serif (Roboto, Arial)
- Display fonts that call attention to themselves
- Script fonts for body text

---

## 5. Mood and Atmosphere

### What the player should feel
- A sense of being on the water — gentle movement, soft reflections
- The warmth of delivering something meaningful
- The quiet satisfaction of reading someone correctly
- Calm, not excitement — the game is soothing, not thrilling

### Visual metaphors
- Light on water
- The moment between rain and sunshine
- A shop owner who knows their regulars
- Old books and fresh flowers

### What NOT to show
- Stress indicators or urgency cues
- High contrast or sharp transitions
- Loud colors competing with the flowers
- Anything that makes the player feel watched or judged

---

## 6. Character / Customer Visual Direction

**Style:** Simple but expressive. Think flat illustration with minimal features — the face is suggestion, not detail. What matters is posture and color, not eyes.

**Approach:** 
- Customers are suggestions of people, not portraits
- Use silhouette and color to communicate mood — a grieving customer feels different from a celebratory one through color warmth and posture, not expression detail
- Clothing should be simple, recognizable silhouettes

**Customer emotional states should read through:**
- Color temperature (warm = open, cool = guarded)
- Posture (open = leaning in, closed = arms crossed)
- A single prop or detail that tells a story

---

## 7. UI Direction

**Overall:** Clean, minimal, unobtrusive. The game is about the delivery, not about the interface.

**Principles:**
- Large touch/click targets — cozy, not fiddly
- Generous spacing — nothing cramped
- Soft corners and shadows — nothing sharp or aggressive
- Card-based UI for flowers and customers — clean, readable, consistent
- Weather indicator subtle — a small icon, not a dramatic banner

**Color use in UI:**
- Flowers and customers are the color — UI should be muted
- Never let the UI compete with the content

---

## 8. Animation and Motion

**Overall:** Gentle, not snappy. Think the pace of a Sunday morning.

**Transitions:**
- Slow fades, not hard cuts
- Cards slide gently, don't pop
- Water should feel like it has presence — subtle ripple or shimmer effect, not static

**Feedback:**
- A gentle pulse or glow when something is selected
- A soft ease-in when a new element appears
- Nothing should bounce or snap — ease curves should feel like slow breathing

---

## 9. Art Production Principles

### Do
- Mood before detail — atmosphere first, polish second
- Restrained color use — let a few colors pop
- Texture as warmth, not noise
- Simple shapes that feel intentional

### Don't
- Add detail just because you can — every element needs to earn its place
- Use saturated colors that shout — this is a quiet game
- Make characters feel expressive through complex facial animation — rely on color temperature and posture
- Fill every pixel — empty space is part of the aesthetic

## 11. Illustration Quality Bar

Bin has set a clear bar: **flowers must look genuinely good, not generic. The boat must have character, not just be serviceable.**

**Flowers:**
- Each flower type must look like a specific, identifiable flower — not a generic colored blob
- Petal shapes, leaf structure, stem character all need to be correct enough to feel real
- Flowers should look *fresh* — not dried, not wilted, not clip-art flat
- Color accuracy matters: lavender is purple-lilac, not just "any purple"

**Boat:**
- Worn wood with character — grain visible, paint history, signs of use
- Not a generic brown rectangle — should feel like a boat someone maintains with care
- Hand-painted signage feel for the flower shop branding
- Scale and proportion should feel real — you could picture yourself standing on it

**Overall:**
- If it looks like it came from a free asset pack, it's not good enough
- The bar is: "I want to look at this flower" — not "I can tell what flower it's supposed to be"
- Fresh and warm, not sterile or generic

---

## 12. Summary

Flower Boat is a **warm, quiet, observational game**. The visual direction should feel like:
- Unpacking meets A Short Hike
- Afternoon light on water
- The satisfaction of noticing something about someone

The colors are warm and natural. The shapes are simple but intentional. The motion is gentle. The players should feel calm, not stimulated.

**Non-negotiable bar:** flowers look genuinely good, boat has character. Everything serves that standard.
