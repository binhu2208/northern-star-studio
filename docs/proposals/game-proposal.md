# Game Proposal - Emotion Card Game

## Overview
- **Project:** Emotion Card Game
- **Genre:** Narrative roguelike deckbuilder / strategy card game
- **Target Platform:** PC first (Steam), with mobile and console evaluation after core validation
- **Target Audience:** Players aged 18-35 who enjoy indie strategy games, deckbuilders, and emotionally resonant narrative experiences

## Unique Selling Point
Emotion Card Game combines deckbuilding strategy with emotional storytelling: emotions are not just theme, but the core resource and combat language. Players build decks around emotional states, relationships, and choices, creating a clearer identity than generic fantasy deckbuilders.

## Core Gameplay
The core loop is:
1. Choose a character/run path
2. Build and refine an emotion-driven deck through encounters
3. Use cards representing feelings, reactions, and coping patterns in tactical battles
4. Make narrative choices that influence future encounters, deck evolution, and character arcs
5. Progress through repeat runs with unlocks, new cards, and deeper story context

This creates a hybrid of replayable roguelike progression and character-centered narrative payoff.

## Market Research
- **Comparable games:** Slay the Spire, Across the Obelisk, Griftlands, Wildfrost, Inscryption, Monster Train
- **Adjacent audience:** Players interested in cozy narrative games, mental health themes, and relationship-driven indies
- **Market opportunity:** The deckbuilder market is crowded, but most titles compete on mechanics first and theme second. Emotion Card Game has room to stand out by pairing proven deckbuilding structure with a distinctive emotional/narrative hook.
- **Trend signal:** Indie players continue to respond well to strong genre fusion, especially when the pitch is immediately understandable and visually distinct.

## Business / Product Requirements
- Clear market positioning around “emotions become strategy”
- PC-first release scope to reduce platform complexity during early development
- Strong visual identity and character differentiation to support marketing
- Replayable core loop with enough depth to satisfy strategy players
- Narrative systems that are lightweight enough to produce efficiently, but meaningful enough to differentiate the game
- Steam page, trailer hooks, and screenshots must communicate both strategy and story in the first impression

## Market Trends Analysis
- Deckbuilders remain viable when they offer a clear hook, strong readability, and good streamer/watchability
- Narrative strategy hybrids are increasingly attractive to players who want more emotional texture than pure systems games
- Saturation risk is real, so the concept must avoid looking like “another indie deckbuilder”
- Art direction and positioning will matter almost as much as mechanics for discoverability

## User Experience Analysis
- Players need immediate clarity on what emotional cards do mechanically
- The UI should make deck state, emotional state, and consequences easy to read
- Early runs must teach the emotional strategy model quickly without overwhelming players
- Story delivery should support gameplay momentum, not interrupt it
- The strongest UX outcome is: “easy to understand, hard to master, emotionally memorable”

## Estimated Timeline
- **Phase 1: Concept + prototype** — validate combat loop, card readability, and emotional mechanics
- **Phase 2: Vertical slice** — finalize art direction, character framing, core encounter structure, and early content
- **Phase 3: Production + launch prep** — expand content, polish balance, prepare marketing assets, and ship PC release

## Budget Estimate
- **Lean indie scope:** Low-to-mid six figures if built by a small core team over a focused production cycle
- Main cost drivers:
  - engineering and combat systems
  - art/UI production
  - writing/content implementation
  - QA/balance testing
  - launch marketing assets

## Recommendation
Proceed as a **PC-first indie strategy title** with a tightly scoped vertical slice. The concept is commercially interesting if the team can prove two things early:
1. the emotional mechanics are genuinely fun, and
2. the presentation clearly differentiates the game from other deckbuilders.
