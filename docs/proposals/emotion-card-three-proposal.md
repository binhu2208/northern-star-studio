# Game Proposal - Emotion Card Three

## Overview
- **Project:** Emotion Card Three
- **Genre:** Narrative roguelike deckbuilder / strategy card game
- **Target Platform:** PC first (Steam), with later evaluation for Switch and mobile if the combat loop and UI prove portable
- **Target Audience:** Players aged 18-35 who enjoy indie deckbuilders, replayable strategy games, and character-driven narrative experiences

## Unique Selling Point
Emotion Card Three builds on the studio’s emotion-card concept by pushing emotional states from theme into system design. The hook is simple and marketable: players do not just collect attacks and defense cards — they build around moods, reactions, bonds, and emotional momentum. That gives the game a cleaner identity than a generic fantasy deckbuilder and leaves room for stronger narrative differentiation.

A third entry also creates a practical business advantage: the team can reuse and refine lessons from previous Emotion Card work instead of inventing an entirely new production pipeline.

## Core Gameplay
The core gameplay loop is:
1. Start a run with a character, emotional profile, and starting deck
2. Progress through branching encounters that mix combat, narrative choices, and deck growth
3. Use emotion-based cards to attack, defend, manipulate state, trigger combos, and shift relationships
4. Adapt to changing emotional states that influence card performance and encounter outcomes
5. Unlock new cards, characters, and story fragments across repeated runs

The intended experience is easy to read on the surface, but deep enough to reward repeat play, planning, and experimentation.

## Market Research
- **Primary comparables:** Slay the Spire, Griftlands, Wildfrost, Monster Train, Inscryption
- **Recent market signal:** Deckbuilders remain commercially viable when they present a very clear hook. Genre leaders still attract audiences, and breakout titles continue to prove there is demand for strong mechanical differentiation.
- **Notable caution:** The space is crowded. Search and discovery trends around deckbuilders show a market full of “games like Slay the Spire,” which means Emotion Card Three cannot win by being merely competent.
- **Opportunity:** A strong emotional/narrative identity can help the game stand out in store capsules, trailers, screenshots, and influencer coverage.
- **Commercial angle:** If the team positions this as a character-driven deckbuilder with memorable emotional mechanics, it can appeal both to core strategy players and adjacent players who normally bounce off colder system-heavy card games.

## Business / Product Requirements
- Position the game clearly around **emotion-as-strategy**, not just emotion-as-theme
- Keep first-release scope tight enough to ship a polished PC version
- Prioritize readability of card effects, emotional states, and combat outcomes
- Build a content pipeline that supports repeatable card creation, encounter authoring, and character expansion
- Ensure art direction is distinctive enough to avoid looking interchangeable with other indie deckbuilders
- Validate marketability early through a short, pitchable vertical slice

## Market Trends Analysis
- Roguelike deckbuilders are still healthy, but the category is saturated and unforgiving to weak positioning
- Successful entries tend to win on one or more of: a novel hook, very strong presentation, excellent readability, or streamer-friendly chaos
- Narrative hybrids continue to perform well when story supports gameplay rather than slowing it down
- Storefront competition means capsule art, one-sentence pitch clarity, and first-impression readability matter almost as much as the combat system itself
- Breakout card games in recent years show players will absolutely try another deckbuilder, but only if it feels meaningfully distinct

## User Experience Analysis
- Players must understand the meaning of each emotional state quickly
- Card text and UI need to communicate both tactical effect and emotional logic without clutter
- Early runs should teach the emotional system with minimal friction
- Narrative choices should reinforce play, not interrupt pacing
- The ideal UX result is: **clear to learn, satisfying to master, emotionally memorable to talk about**

## Estimated Timeline
- **Phase 1: Concept validation (2-4 weeks)** — finalize core pitch, combat pillars, emotional state model, and competitive positioning
- **Phase 2: Prototype (4-8 weeks)** — build playable combat loop, basic card set, placeholder UI, and first encounter flow
- **Phase 3: Vertical slice (6-10 weeks)** — establish art direction, one polished character path, clearer presentation, and marketing-ready footage
- **Phase 4: Production planning (2-3 weeks)** — scope content, team workload, milestones, and launch path

## Budget Estimate
- **Prototype / vertical slice scope:** low five figures if kept lean and internally focused
- **Small production scope:** low-to-mid six figures depending on art ambition, content volume, and development duration
- **Main cost drivers:**
  - engineering for combat systems and tools
  - UI/UX and card readability work
  - art production and visual identity
  - writing / narrative implementation
  - QA, balancing, and iteration time

## Risks
- **Genre saturation:** the game may be ignored if the pitch is not sharp enough
- **Theme-mechanic mismatch:** emotional framing may feel cosmetic if systems do not reinforce it deeply
- **Content burden:** narrative card games can become expensive if the content pipeline is not disciplined
- **Readability risk:** emotional systems can become confusing if too many states or modifiers stack at once

## Recommendation
Proceed with Emotion Card Three as a **PC-first vertical-slice candidate**.

The project is worth pursuing if the team can prove three things early:
1. emotional mechanics are strategically fun, not just novel on paper
2. the game reads clearly in screenshots and short gameplay clips
3. the project can be scoped tightly enough to avoid content sprawl

If those three checks land, Emotion Card Three has a credible path to becoming a distinctive indie deckbuilder rather than another interchangeable genre entry.
