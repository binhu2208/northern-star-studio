# Controlled Burn Reference Board

**Character:** Ember  
**Family:** Fire  
**Phase:** 5 — Controlled Burn  
**Goal:** Define the visual mood for Ember's Phase 5 batch so the art reads as **disciplined heat, tempered force, and strategic control** rather than chaotic destruction or Phase 6 rebirth.

This batch should feel like heat that has been **contained, aimed, and made useful**. The emotional read is not "explosion" and not "transcendence." It is the moment where anger becomes a tool: banked coals, forge heat, steady flame, guarded intensity.

---

## 1) Visual Motifs

Phase 5 imagery should center on **contained fire systems** rather than wildfire spectacle. Think in terms of fire being held, shaped, or redirected.

- **Banked bonfire cores** — low, dense, hot centers rather than towering flame columns
- **Forge and hearth language** — ironwork, anvil heat, contained furnaces, vented embers
- **Tempered structures** — fire inside braziers, pits, chimneys, channels, or cracked stone enclosures
- **Controlled venting** — smoke trails, breath-like heat release, sparks moving in deliberate arcs instead of chaotic spray
- **Heat as stored potential** — glowing seams, heated metal edges, ember beds, coal-red interiors under darker outer shells
- **Protective utility** — shield-like shapes, fire used to reinforce, harden, or preserve rather than only attack
- **Tension without rupture** — imagery that feels pressurized and focused, but not seconds away from detonation
- **Human-scale burn zones** — intimate, readable, practical fire sources over giant fantasy infernos

**Search / mood-board keywords:**
- banked campfire coals
- blacksmith forge glow
- charcoal kiln heat
- tempered steel coloration
- ember bed close-up
- furnace vent light
- brazier with low flame
- controlled burn forestry lines
- soot-stained firebrick
- heat shimmer over metal

---

## 2) Lighting Cues

Lighting should communicate **discipline and retention**. Use concentrated warm light with clear falloff, not all-over orange wash.

- Favor **localized heat sources** with strong focal direction: forge mouths, coal beds, brazier bowls, heated blade edges
- Use **deep surrounding shadow** so the fire feels contained and purposeful, not ambient everywhere
- Keep highlights **dense and selective** — hot whites and yellow-orange accents only at the hottest points
- Let most of the palette live in **ember reds, furnace oranges, smoked umbers, iron blacks, and ash neutrals**
- Introduce **subtle heat bloom** and shimmer, but avoid magical radiance halos that imply divine ascension or phoenix energy
- Rim light can work if it feels like **reflected forge light**, not heroic backlight
- Value grouping should stay bold enough to read at card size: one clear hot focal zone, one support zone, one dark containment mass
- Firelight should feel **practical and weighty**, almost industrial, not whimsical or decorative

---

## 3) Material / Surface Cues

Surface treatment is where this phase really separates itself from generic fantasy fire art. Everything should feel **worked, heated, used, and slightly scarred**.

- **Oxidized metal** with blue-brown heat temper colors near edges and impact zones
- **Sooted stone and firebrick** with powdery ash deposits and hairline heat cracks
- **Charred wood** that still holds structure — blackened exterior, dim red life within splits and seams
- **Hammered steel / forged surfaces** that catch small bands of hot reflection rather than mirror shine
- **Ash, clinker, coal dust, and slag** as grounding texture elements
- **Ceramic, iron, and brick containment materials** over ornate fantasy crystal or magical fuel sources
- **Dry, matte, scorched textures** should dominate; use glossy molten accents sparingly so they stay special
- **Heat wear** should read as history: repeated use, not one catastrophic burn event

This phase should feel tactile and believable: a place where fire has been managed long enough to leave marks.

---

## 4) Composition Do's / Don'ts

Phase 5 card compositions should read quickly in the 3:2 card crop and support the batch pipeline rule of one strong focal point per image.

### Do
- Build around **one contained heat focal point**: ember core, forge mouth, heated weapon, shielded burn line
- Use **framing elements** like anvils, stone rings, metal braces, or dark silhouettes to visually contain the fire
- Favor **mid-close compositions** that make heat texture readable at card size
- Let shapes feel **stable, braced, and intentional** — triangular, anchored, or horizontally grounded compositions work well
- Show the relationship between **pressure and restraint**: heat inside a vessel, force inside a stance, glow inside a crack
- Keep negative space dark and clean so the hot areas feel more intense
- Vary batch shots between object-focused, gesture-focused, and environmental close-ups without losing the same controlled-burn mood

### Don't
- Don't stage fire as a giant background spectacle that swallows the frame
- Don't use soaring silhouettes, wing-like flame shapes, or upward rebirth poses that drift toward Phase 6 phoenix imagery
- Don't fill every edge with sparks, smoke curls, and VFX noise; it weakens the sense of control
- Don't default to generic red-orange fantasy fire gradients without material context
- Don't center every composition on screaming faces or explosive action beats; this phase is mastery, not rage-spike chaos
- Don't over-render busy backgrounds that compete with the heat focal point in card view

---

## What to Avoid

Avoid anything that turns this batch into **generic fire fantasy**, **boss-mode inferno art**, or **phoenix rebirth symbolism**. Phase 5 is not about total destruction, magical ascension, angelic flame wings, explosive spellcasting, lava-castle spectacle, or triumphant rising-from-ashes imagery. If a piece looks like Ember has become a mythic fire god, it has drifted too far. The right lane is narrower and stronger: grounded heat, forged restraint, soot, iron, ember cores, and deliberate intensity that feels useful, survivable, and earned.
